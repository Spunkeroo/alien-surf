How to Choose a Wetsuit: Complete Size & Thickness Guide 2026

A wetsuit is the second most important piece of surf equipment after your surfboard. The right wetsuit keeps you warm enough to surf for hours, flexible enough to paddle and pop up, and comfortable enough that you forget you are wearing it. The wrong wetsuit either leaves you shivering after 20 minutes or restricts your movement so much that paddling becomes a workout.

This guide covers everything you need to know about choosing a wetsuit in 2026: how thickness relates to water temperature, how to read size charts, the difference between entry-level and premium neoprene, zipper types, seam construction, and specific brand recommendations at every price point.

How Wetsuits Work

A wetsuit does not keep you dry -- it keeps you warm by trapping a thin layer of water between your skin and the neoprene. Your body heat warms this water layer, which then acts as insulation. The neoprene itself contains millions of tiny nitrogen gas bubbles that provide additional insulation, much like a down jacket traps air.

This is why fit matters so much. If the wetsuit is too loose, cold water constantly flushes through, replacing the warm layer your body has heated. If it is too tight, it restricts blood flow and movement, making you cold and fatigued faster. The ideal fit is snug against the skin everywhere, with no bunching, sagging, or air pockets, but loose enough to allow full range of motion in the shoulders and hips.

Thickness Guide by Water Temperature

Wetsuit thickness is expressed as two numbers (e.g., 3/2mm or 4/3mm). The first number is the torso thickness, the second is the arms and legs. The torso is thicker because your core needs the most insulation, while thinner limbs provide better flexibility for paddling.

Personal Cold Tolerance Varies

These are guidelines, not rules. Some people run warm and are comfortable in a 3/2mm at 58F. Others feel cold in a 4/3mm at 65F. If you are between sizes or thicknesses, go warmer. Being too warm in a wetsuit is mildly uncomfortable. Being too cold ends your session early and can be dangerous.

Wetsuit Types Explained

Full Suit (Steamer)

Covers your entire body from wrists to ankles. The most common type for surfing in most climates. Available in 2mm through 6mm+ thicknesses. A 3/2mm full suit is the most versatile and widely purchased wetsuit globally.

Spring Suit (Shorty)

Short arms (usually sleeveless or short sleeves) and short legs (above the knee). Designed for warm water where a full suit is too hot but a rash guard is not enough. Typically 2mm thickness. Popular for summer surfing in temperate climates.

Long-Sleeve Spring Suit

Full-length arms with short legs. Provides more upper body warmth than a standard spring suit while keeping legs free for mobility. Good for transitional seasons.

Farmer John / Jane

Sleeveless full suit (no arms, full legs). Uncommon in surfing but sometimes used in warm water when leg protection is needed (e.g., surfing over reef).

How to Find Your Size

Wetsuit sizing is based on height and weight, with chest and waist measurements as secondary factors. Every brand has its own size chart, and they differ significantly -- a Medium in O'Neill is not the same as a Medium in Rip Curl.

If your measurements fall between two sizes, choose the smaller size. Neoprene stretches 10-15% with use. A slightly tight new wetsuit will break in to a perfect fit; a slightly loose new wetsuit will become baggy and flush cold water.

Trying On a Wetsuit

When trying on a wetsuit in a shop, check these fit points:

  • Neck: Snug but not choking. You should be able to breathe normally. No gap between neck and neoprene.
  • Armpits: No bunching or excess material. Raise your arms overhead -- this should feel snug but possible.
  • Lower back: No gap or bubble of air when you bend forward. This is the most common area for cold water entry.
  • Crotch: The crotch seam should sit at your body, not hanging low. Low crotch = restriction in legs.
  • Wrists and ankles: Seal snugly against skin with no gaps.
  • Overall: No air pockets, no loose panels, no restriction when squatting or extending arms.

Zipper Types: Front vs. Back vs. Zipperless

Back Zip

A long zipper runs from the lower back to the upper neck. This is the traditional design and remains the easiest to get on and off. The zipper flap lies flat against your back and is sealed by a Velcro neck closure. Back-zip suits are ideal for beginners and for warm water where maximum seal is less critical. The downside: the zipper is the least flexible and least waterproof part of the suit, and it runs right down the area that flexes most during paddling.

Front Zip (Chest Zip)

A short zipper runs horizontally across the upper chest. Entry is through a neck opening that you stretch over your head. Front-zip suits are warmer (the back panel is a continuous piece of neoprene with no zipper seam), more flexible (no zipper restricting back movement), and leak less water. The trade-off is that they are harder to get on and off, especially when new. Front-zip is the preferred choice for cold water surfing and is now the most common design among intermediate and advanced surfers.

Zipperless

No zipper at all. Entry is through an oversized neck opening that relies on stretch neoprene to seal once on. Zipperless suits offer maximum flexibility and warmth but are the most difficult to put on. They are less common and typically found only in premium wetsuit lines.

Seam Construction

Flatlock Seams

Panels are stitched together with thread that passes through both sides of the neoprene, creating a flat, comfortable seam. The holes allow water to seep through, making flatlock seams suitable only for warm water suits (2mm spring suits). They are the most comfortable and cheapest to produce.

Glued and Blindstitched (GBS)

Panels are first glued together, then stitched from one side only (the needle does not penetrate all the way through). This creates a waterproof seam that does not leak. GBS is the standard construction for all full suits and the minimum you should accept for any suit designed for water below 68F (20C).

Sealed and Taped

GBS seams with an additional layer of liquid sealant or neoprene tape applied over the interior seam. This provides maximum waterproofing and durability. Found on mid-range to premium wetsuits and recommended for cold water use.

Neoprene Quality and Materials

Not all neoprene is created equal. The quality of the neoprene determines how warm, flexible, light, and durable the wetsuit is. This is the primary factor that separates a $100 wetsuit from a $400 wetsuit.

  • Standard neoprene: Found in budget wetsuits. Heavier, stiffer, and less insulating per millimeter. Adequate for warm water and occasional use.
  • Premium neoprene (e.g., O'Neill TechnoButter, Rip Curl E7 Flash Lining): Lighter, stretchier, warmer, and more durable. Found in mid-range to premium suits. The difference in flexibility is dramatic -- premium neoprene feels like a second skin compared to standard.
  • Limestone-based neoprene: Most premium neoprene is now derived from limestone rather than petroleum, resulting in lighter weight and more consistent cell structure. Brands like Patagonia have pioneered plant-based Yulex neoprene as a more sustainable alternative.
  • Interior lining: Quick-dry thermal linings (like Rip Curl's FlashDry or O'Neill's TechnoButter Air) trap heat and dry faster between sessions. They make a noticeable difference in warmth and comfort.

Wetsuit Care & Maintenance

Proper care dramatically extends your wetsuit's lifespan. Follow these practices after every session:

  1. Rinse with fresh water immediately after each session. Salt, sand, and UV exposure are the three things that degrade neoprene fastest.
  2. Turn inside out and rinse the interior to flush salt from the lining.
  3. Dry in shade. Never leave your wetsuit in direct sunlight. UV radiation breaks down neoprene. Hang it inside-out on a wide hanger or over a railing -- not by the shoulders on a thin hanger, which stretches the neoprene.
  4. Do not use hot water, bleach, or a washing machine. Lukewarm or cold fresh water only.
  5. Store hanging or loosely folded in a cool, dry place. Do not fold tightly or compress for extended periods, which creates permanent creases.
  6. Use wetsuit shampoo once a month to remove bacteria, odor, and salt buildup. Brands like Piss Off, Wetsuit Wash, and Rip Curl's Wetsuit Bomb work well.

Frequently Asked Questions

How tight should a wetsuit be?

A properly fitting wetsuit should feel snug everywhere with no loose fabric or air pockets, but should not restrict your breathing or range of motion. You should be able to raise your arms above your head and squat without the suit pulling uncomfortably. A new wetsuit should feel tight -- neoprene stretches 10-15% after a few sessions.

What thickness wetsuit do I need?

For water above 72F (22C), a 2mm spring suit or rash guard is sufficient. For 62-72F, use 3/2mm. For 52-62F, use 4/3mm. For 42-52F, use 5/4mm with boots. Below 42F, a 6/5mm suit with hood, boots, and gloves is necessary.

How long does a wetsuit last?

A quality wetsuit lasts 2-4 years with regular use (3-5 sessions per week) if properly maintained. Budget wetsuits may last only 1-2 years. Proper rinsing, shade drying, and correct storage are the key factors.

Should I buy a front-zip or back-zip wetsuit?

Front-zip suits are warmer, more flexible, and leak less water. Back-zip suits are easier to get on and off. For cold water (below 60F), front-zip is strongly recommended. For warm water, either works.

How much should I spend on a wetsuit?

Budget $100-200 for basic warm-water suits. Mid-range ($200-350) offers better neoprene and construction. Premium ($350-550) uses the lightest, stretchiest materials. For occasional warm-water surfing, $100-150 is fine. For regular cold-water surfing, invest $250+.

Conclusion

Choosing the right wetsuit comes down to three decisions: thickness (determined by water temperature), size (determined by your body measurements), and budget (determined by how often you surf and how cold the water is). Get these three right, and you will have a wetsuit that keeps you warm, moves with you, and lasts for years.

Start with the water temperature where you surf most often. Choose the corresponding thickness from the chart. Try on multiple brands to find the best fit for your body. And invest in quality if you surf regularly in cold water -- a premium wetsuit is one of the best value-for-money upgrades in all of surfing.
